## Detailed Identification & Botanical Information

### [IMG_5584.JPG](img/IMG_5584.JPG) & [IMG_5586.JPG](img/IMG_5586.JPG) & [IMG_5612.JPG](img/IMG_5612.JPG): Bush Honeysuckle (*Lonicera* spp. / *Lonicera tatarica*)
* **Common Name**: Bush Honeysuckle / Tatarian Honeysuckle
* **Scientific Name**: *Lonicera* spp. (likely *Lonicera tatarica*)
* **Description**: An upright, deciduous shrub growing up to 10-15 feet tall. It has opposite, simple, oval leaves with entire margins and hollow stems. In late spring, it produces pink or white flowers which develop into paired, shiny red or orange berries in summer.
* **Interesting Facts**:
  * **Invasive Species**: Introduced to North America from East Asia in the 1800s for landscaping and soil stabilization. It is now a severe forest invasive that leafs out earlier than native species, shading out native understory spring ephemerals.
  * **Ecological Impact**: Birds readily consume the colorful berries and disperse seeds. However, the berries are high in carbohydrates but low in fat and protein compared to native berries, providing poor nutritional energy for migrating birds.

### [IMG_5585.JPG](img/IMG_5585.JPG) & [IMG_5600.JPG](img/IMG_5600.JPG): Wild Bergamot / Bee Balm (*Monarda fistulosa*)
* **Common Name**: Wild Bergamot / Bee Balm
* **Scientific Name**: *Monarda fistulosa*
* **Description**: A native perennial wildflower in the mint family (Lamiaceae), growing 2 to 4 feet tall with square stems and opposite, lanceolate, serrated leaves. The flowers are tubular, lavender-to-pink, arranged in dense, terminal, rounded heads.
* **Interesting Facts**:
  * **Medicinal Uses**: Native Americans used it extensively in tea to treat colds, respiratory ailments, and stomach pain due to its high concentration of thymol, which has antiseptic properties.
  * **Pollinator Magnet**: It is highly attractive to a wide array of pollinators, including bumblebees, hummingbirds, and sphinx moths.

---

### [IMG_5587.JPG](img/IMG_5587.JPG): Swamp Milkweed (*Asclepias incarnata*)
* **Common Name**: Swamp Milkweed
* **Scientific Name**: *Asclepias incarnata*
* **Description**: A native wetland perennial growing 3 to 5 feet tall. It features opposite, narrow lanceolate leaves and dense clusters of pink-to-purple, fragrant, crown-like flowers. Like all milkweeds, it has milky sap containing cardiac glycosides.
* **Interesting Facts**:
  * **Monarch Butterfly Host**: It is a critical host plant for the Monarch butterfly (*Danaus plexippus*). Monarch caterpillars feed exclusively on milkweed leaves, bioaccumulating the plant's toxic cardiac glycosides, which makes the caterpillars and subsequent adult butterflies toxic to avian predators.
  * **Wetland Adaptability**: Unlike Common Milkweed (*Asclepias syriaca*), Swamp Milkweed is highly adapted to wet, clay-heavy soils, making it a key restoration species for pond edges and rain gardens.

### [IMG_5588.JPG](img/IMG_5588.JPG) & [IMG_5600.JPG](img/IMG_5600.JPG): Daisy Fleabane (*Erigeron annuus*)
* **Common Name**: Daisy Fleabane
* **Scientific Name**: *Erigeron annuus*
* **Description**: A native annual or biennial herbaceous wildflower with alternate, coarsely-toothed leaves and numerous daisy-like flower heads. The flower heads consist of 40-100 very thin, white or pale-pink ray florets surrounding a bright yellow center of disk florets.
* **Interesting Facts**:
  * **Historical Repellent**: The common name "fleabane" originates from the historic belief that drying and burning the plant would repel fleas, gnats, and other household pests. It was frequently stuffed into mattresses for this purpose.
  * **Pioneer Species**: It colonizes disturbed soils rapidly and is an important early-season nectar source for small bees, hoverflies, and wasps.

---

### [IMG_5589.JPG](img/IMG_5589.JPG): Missouri Gooseberry (*Ribes missouriense*)
* **Common Name**: Missouri Gooseberry
* **Scientific Name**: *Ribes missouriense*
* **Description**: A native, spiny deciduous shrub growing up to 3-5 feet tall. It has palmately lobed, toothed leaves and sharp spines at the twig nodes. The flowers are pale yellow with long, protruding stamens, ripening into smooth, spherical berries that turn from green to reddish-purple.
* **Interesting Facts**:
  * **Sharp Armor**: The sharp nodal spines make it an excellent refuge plant for small birds and mammals looking to escape larger predators.
  * **Culinary Use**: The tart, edible berries are harvested to make delicious pies, jams, jellies, and wines.

---

### [IMG_5590.JPG](img/IMG_5590.JPG): Eastern Red Cedar (*Juniperus virginiana*)
* **Common Name**: Eastern Red Cedar
* **Scientific Name**: *Juniperus virginiana*
* **Description**: A dense, slow-growing evergreen tree that is actually a species of juniper. It has scale-like, aromatic needles and reddish-brown bark that shreds in thin strips. Female trees produce berry-like, waxy blue cones containing seeds.
* **Interesting Facts**:
  * **Rot & Insect Resistant**: The wood contains aromatic natural oils that repel insects (especially moths) and prevent rot, making it highly prized for lining chests, wardrobes, and crafting fence posts.
  * **Ecological Pioneer**: It is a pioneer species that is one of the first trees to colonize abandoned fields and dry rocky slopes. The blue "berries" are a critical winter food source for the Cedar Waxwing.

---

### [IMG_5591.JPG](img/IMG_5591.JPG) & [IMG_5595.JPG](img/IMG_5595.JPG) & [IMG_5601.JPG](img/IMG_5601.JPG): Gray Dogwood (*Cornus racemosa*)
* **Common Name**: Gray Dogwood
* **Scientific Name**: *Cornus racemosa*
* **Description**: A native deciduous shrub that grows up to 10-15 feet tall, forming thickets. It has opposite, entire, lanceolate leaves with distinctive arcuate (curved) veins. In late spring, it produces flat-topped clusters of white flowers that ripen into white berries on bright red stalks (pedicels).
* **Interesting Facts**:
  * **Persistent Color**: After birds consume the white berries in late summer, the bright red pedicels persist through the winter, providing colorful winter interest.
  * **Wildlife Support**: Over 100 species of birds feed on the high-fat berries, making it highly valuable for wildlife restoration.

---

### [IMG_5592.JPG](img/IMG_5592.JPG): Chokecherry (*Prunus virginiana*)
* **Common Name**: Chokecherry
* **Scientific Name**: *Prunus virginiana*
* **Description**: A native suckering shrub or small tree growing up to 20-30 feet tall. It has alternate, finely-serrated obovate leaves. White flowers are arranged in long, cylindrical terminal clusters (racemes), ripening into shiny dark red or black cherries.
* **Interesting Facts**:
  * **Cyanogenic Glycosides**: The leaves, twigs, and fruit seeds contain amygdalin, a compound that breaks down into toxic hydrogen cyanide when chewed, making them toxic to livestock.
  * **Astringent Edible**: The raw fruit is highly astringent and will dry out the mouth (hence the name "chokecherry"), but cooking it neutralizes the bitterness, making it popular for syrups, jellies, and pemmican.

---

### [IMG_5593.JPG](img/IMG_5593.JPG): Broadleaf Cattail (*Typha latifolia*)
* **Common Name**: Broadleaf Cattail
* **Scientific Name**: *Typha latifolia*
* **Description**: A native semi-aquatic perennial plant growing 3 to 10 feet tall in wet soils. It has long, flat, strap-like leaves and a distinctive double-spiked inflorescence: a male pollen-bearing spike above a dense, velvety-brown female seed-producing cylinder.
* **Interesting Facts**:
  * **Survivalist Supermarket**: Nearly every part of the cattail is edible. In spring, the young shoots can be eaten raw or cooked; in early summer, the green flower spikes can be boiled like corn; in autumn, the starchy underground rhizomes can be dried and ground into flour.
  * **Water Purifier**: Cattails are natural biofilters, capable of absorbing heavy metals and excess agricultural nutrients from runoff, improving water quality in wetlands.

---

### [IMG_5595.JPG](img/IMG_5595.JPG) & [IMG_5614.JPG](img/IMG_5614.JPG): Riverbank Grape (*Vitis riparia*)
* **Common Name**: Riverbank Grape / Frost Grape
* **Scientific Name**: *Vitis riparia*
* **Description**: A vigorous, native woody climbing vine that can climb over 50 feet. It features alternate, deeply-lobed, sharply-toothed leaves and climbs using branched tendrils. It produces small, fragrant, greenish-yellow flowers and hanging clusters of dark-purple grapes with a whitish bloom.
* **Interesting Facts**:
  * **Grafted Savior**: Riverbank Grape is highly resistant to phylloxera, a microscopic insect pest that devastated European grapevines in the late 19th century. European grapevines (*Vitis vinifera*) were saved by grafting them onto the rootstocks of native American grapes like *Vitis riparia*.
  * **Cold Hardiness**: It is one of the most cold-hardy grape species, surviving temperatures down to -50°F (-45°C), and has been used extensively in breeding hardy hybrid wine grapes.

---

### [IMG_5597.JPG](img/IMG_5597.JPG): Common Evening Primrose (*Oenothera biennis*)
* **Common Name**: Common Evening Primrose
* **Scientific Name**: *Oenothera biennis*
* **Description**: A native biennial herbaceous plant. In its second year, it produces a tall, erect stem (3-6 feet) with alternate, lanceolate leaves and a spike of bright yellow, four-petaled flowers that open at dusk and close by noon the next day.
* **Interesting Facts**:
  * **Nocturnal Pollination**: Because the flowers open in the evening, they are primarily pollinated by night-flying insects like sphinx moths and hoverflies.
  * **Medicinal Oil**: The seeds contain high amounts of gamma-linolenic acid (GLA), an essential fatty acid. Evening primrose oil is commonly sold as a dietary supplement to treat inflammatory conditions and premenstrual syndrome.

---

### [IMG_5598.JPG](img/IMG_5598.JPG): Oxeye Daisy (*Leucanthemum vulgare*)
* **Common Name**: Oxeye Daisy
* **Scientific Name**: *Leucanthemum vulgare*
* **Description**: An introduced perennial herbaceous plant growing 1 to 2 feet tall. It has lobed basal leaves and erect stems terminating in a single flower head with 20-30 white ray florets and a flat, yellow disk of center florets.
* **Interesting Facts**:
  * **Invasive Seed Producer**: Introduced from Europe as an ornamental plant, it has become invasive in pastures and fields. A single plant can produce up to 26,000 seeds that remain viable in the soil for up to 40 years.
  * **Pasture Pest**: Dairy cows that graze on Oxeye Daisy produce milk with an off-flavor, and the plant is generally avoided by livestock, causing it to take over grazing lands.

---

### [IMG_5599.JPG](img/IMG_5599.JPG): Wild Black Raspberry (*Rubus occidentalis*)
* **Common Name**: Wild Black Raspberry
* **Scientific Name**: *Rubus occidentalis*
* **Description**: A native deciduous shrub with arching, thorny canes that are covered in a whitish, waxy bloom (glaucous coating). The leaves are pinnately compound with three (rarely five) leaflets. It produces white flowers and sweet, hollow, dark purple-black aggregate fruits.
* **Interesting Facts**:
  * **Hollow Fruit**: When picked, the black raspberry pulls away from the central receptacle (the "core"), leaving a hollow, cup-like fruit. This distinguishes raspberries from blackberries, where the receptacle remains inside the harvested fruit.
  * **Tip Rooting**: The long arching canes have the unique ability to root at the tips when they touch the ground, allowing the plant to spread vegetatively and form dense, thorny brambles.

---

### [IMG_5600.JPG](img/IMG_5600.JPG): Virginia Creeper (*Parthenocissus quinquefolia*)
* **Common Name**: Virginia Creeper
* **Scientific Name**: *Parthenocissus quinquefolia*
* **Description**: A native, woody deciduous vine that climbs or acts as a ground cover. It is easily identified by its palmately compound leaves with five leaflets. It climbs using tendrils tipped with small, adhesive pads.
* **Interesting Facts**:
  * **Wall Climber**: The adhesive pads on its tendrils secrete a strong natural glue that allows it to climb smooth concrete and brick walls without penetrating or damaging the mortar (unlike English Ivy).
  * **Autumn Display**: Its leaves turn a brilliant, fiery crimson in the fall, making it a popular native alternative for autumn landscaping.

---

### [IMG_5602.JPG](img/IMG_5602.JPG): Bittersweet (*Celastrus* spp.)
* **Common Name**: Bittersweet (likely Oriental Bittersweet, *Celastrus orbiculatus*)
* **Scientific Name**: *Celastrus* spp.
* **Description**: A deciduous woody climbing vine with alternate, roundish leaves and small greenish flowers. In autumn, it produces showy yellow capsules that split open to reveal bright orange-red fleshy seed coverings (arils).
* **Interesting Facts**:
  * **Girdling Habit**: Oriental Bittersweet is a highly destructive invasive species that climbs by twining tightly around tree trunks. As the tree grows, the vine constricts its growth (girdling), eventually cutting off the tree's vascular flow and killing it.
  * **Native vs. Exotic**: It is closely related to the native American Bittersweet (*Celastrus scandens*), but the invasive Oriental variety produces flowers/fruit in the leaf axils along the stem, whereas the native produces them only at the branch tips.

---

### [IMG_5610.JPG](img/IMG_5610.JPG): Peach-leaved Willow (*Salix amygdaloides*)
* **Common Name**: Peach-leaved Willow
* **Scientific Name**: *Salix amygdaloides*
* **Description**: A native deciduous tree growing up to 30-50 feet tall, often multi-stemmed. It has alternate, narrow lanceolate leaves that resemble peach leaves, with pale/whitish undersides and finely-serrated margins.
* **Interesting Facts**:
  * **Aspirin Ancestry**: Willow bark contains salicin, a chemical precursor to salicylic acid. Indigenous peoples and ancient cultures chewed willow bark to relieve pain and reduce fevers, which eventually led to the development of synthetic aspirin.
  * **Soil Stabilizer**: Its aggressive, fibrous root systems are highly effective at holding stream and pond banks together, preventing erosion during high water events.

---

### [IMG_5613.JPG](img/IMG_5613.JPG): Golden Alexanders (*Zizia aurea*)
* **Common Name**: Golden Alexanders
* **Scientific Name**: *Zizia aurea*
* **Description**: A native perennial wildflower in the carrot family (Apiaceae), growing 1 to 3 feet tall. It has 2-3 times ternately compound leaves with serrated margins. The bright yellow flowers are arranged in flat-topped compound umbels.
* **Interesting Facts**:
  * **Swallowtail Host**: This plant is a critical host for the caterpillars of the Black Swallowtail butterfly (*Papilio polyxenes*), which feed on the leaves.
  * **Early Bloomer**: Unlike many other carrot family members that bloom in late summer, Golden Alexanders bloom in late spring, providing a critical early nectar source for short-tongued native bees.

---

### [IMG_5614.JPG](img/IMG_5614.JPG): American Elderberry (*Sambucus canadensis*)
* **Common Name**: American Elderberry
* **Scientific Name**: *Sambucus canadensis*
* **Description**: A native deciduous shrub growing up to 10-12 feet tall. It features opposite, pinnately compound leaves with 5-11 serrated leaflets. In early summer, it produces large, flat-topped clusters (cymes) of small, fragrant white flowers, followed by tiny, drooping clusters of dark purple-black berries.
* **Interesting Facts**:
  * **Immune Booster**: The cooked berries are rich in Vitamin C, flavonoids, and antioxidants, and are widely used in herbal medicine to treat flu and cold symptoms.
  * **Toxic Raw**: Raw elderberries, as well as the leaves, stems, and roots of the plant, contain cyanogenic glycosides and can cause severe stomach upset if eaten raw. Cooking the berries renders them perfectly safe.

---

### [IMG_5615.JPG](img/IMG_5615.JPG): Crabapple (*Malus* spp.)
* **Common Name**: Crabapple
* **Scientific Name**: *Malus* spp.
* **Description**: Small, deciduous trees in the rose family (Rosaceae) with alternate, oval leaves and showy spring blossoms. They produce small pomes (apples) that are less than 2 inches in diameter.
* **Interesting Facts**:
  * **Size Definition**: The sole distinction between a "crabapple" and a standard "apple" is the fruit size. If the fruit is 2 inches (5 cm) or less in diameter, it is classified as a crabapple; if larger, it is a table apple.
  * **Cross-Pollination**: Crabapples are excellent universal pollinators for commercial apple orchards because they produce massive quantities of pollen and bloom over an extended period.

---

### [IMG_5616.JPG](img/IMG_5616.JPG) & [IMG_5617.JPG](img/IMG_5617.JPG): White Sweet Clover (*Melilotus albus*)
* **Common Name**: White Sweet Clover
* **Scientific Name**: *Melilotus albus*
* **Description**: An introduced biennial herb growing 3 to 6 feet tall. It has alternate, trifoliate (three-parted) leaves and produces long, slender racemes of small, white, pea-like flowers. It has a sweet vanilla-like fragrance when crushed.
* **Interesting Facts**:
  * **Beekeeper Favorite**: Highly valued by beekeepers, it produces abundant nectar and is a major source of high-quality commercial clover honey.
  * **Soil Builder**: Like other legumes, sweet clover has symbiotic root bacteria that fix atmospheric nitrogen into the soil, which was historically used as green manure to improve soil fertility.

---

### [IMG_5618.JPG](img/IMG_5618.JPG): Green Ash (*Fraxinus pennsylvanica*)
* **Common Name**: Green Ash
* **Scientific Name**: *Fraxinus pennsylvanica*
* **Description**: A native deciduous tree growing 50 to 70 feet tall with opposite, pinnately compound leaves consisting of 5 to 9 lanceolate, finely-serrated leaflets. The bark has a distinctive diamond-shaped furrowed pattern on mature trees.
* **Interesting Facts**:
  * **Emerald Ash Borer Threat**: Green Ash is currently facing ecological devastation across North America due to the Emerald Ash Borer (*Agrilus planipennis*), an invasive Asian beetle whose larvae feed on the inner bark, girdling and killing the trees.
  * **Wood Use**: Due to its strength, flexibility, and straight grain, ash wood has been traditionally used to make baseball bats, tool handles, oars, and electric guitar bodies.

---

### [IMG_5619.JPG](img/IMG_5619.JPG): Mulberry (*Morus* spp. / *Morus alba*)
* **Common Name**: White Mulberry / Mulberry
* **Scientific Name**: *Morus* spp. (likely *Morus alba*)
* **Description**: A fast-growing tree with alternate leaves that are highly variable in shape (some are lobed, others are not) and have shiny upper surfaces. It produces hanging aggregate fruits that resemble blackberries, turning from green/white to red to dark purple.
* **Interesting Facts**:
  * **Silkworm Diet**: The White Mulberry is native to China and was introduced to North America in the colonial era in an attempt to establish a silk industry, as its leaves are the sole food source of the silkworm caterpillar.
  * **Invasive Hybridizer**: White Mulberry is now a widespread invasive species that outcompetes and hybridizes with the native Red Mulberry (*Morus rubra*), threatening the genetic purity of the native species.

---

### [IMG_5620.JPG](img/IMG_5620.JPG) & [IMG_5621.JPG](img/IMG_5621.JPG): Yellow Archangel (*Lamium galeobdolon*)
* **Common Name**: Yellow Archangel / Golden Deadnettle
* **Scientific Name**: *Lamium galeobdolon* (or *Galeobdolon luteum*)
* **Description**: An introduced, invasive perennial ground cover in the mint family (Lamiaceae). It features square stems and opposite, ovate leaves with coarsely-toothed margins and striking silver/metallic variegation. It produces yellow, hooded flowers in spring.
* **Interesting Facts**:
  * **Garden Escapee**: Originally planted as a shade-tolerant ground cover in gardens, it easily escapes cultivation and aggressively invades woodlands, forming dense mats that exclude native plants.
  * **Rapid Spread**: It spreads vegetatively via trailing stolons (runners) that root at nodes, and is extremely difficult to remove because any stem fragment left in the soil can regrow.

---

### [IMG_5622.JPG](img/IMG_5622.JPG): Eastern Cottonwood (*Populus deltoides*)
* **Common Name**: Eastern Cottonwood
* **Scientific Name**: *Populus deltoides*
* **Description**: A massive, fast-growing native deciduous tree. It has alternate, triangular (deltate) leaves with coarse, curved teeth and flattened petioles (leaf stems) that cause the leaves to flutter in the slightest breeze.
* **Interesting Facts**:
  * **Fluttering Leaves**: The flat petiole acts like a hinge, allowing the leaves to sway from side to side in the wind. This reduces wind resistance, helping the tree's branches survive heavy storms without breaking.
  * **Cotton Seed Fluff**: In late spring, female trees release millions of tiny seeds enclosed in cottony fluff, which drift on the wind like snow.

---

### [IMG_5623.JPG](img/IMG_5623.JPG): Burdock (*Arctium* spp. / *Arctium minus*)
* **Common Name**: Lesser Burdock / Common Burdock
* **Scientific Name**: *Arctium* spp. (likely *Arctium minus*)
* **Description**: An introduced biennial weed that forms a large basal rosette of massive, heart-shaped, wavy-edged leaves in its first year. In the second year, it produces a tall flowering stem with purple thistle-like flowers encased in hooked bracts (burs).
* **Interesting Facts**:
  * **Inspiration for Velcro**: In 1941, Swiss engineer George de Mestral went for a walk with his dog and noticed burdock burs clinging to his socks and the dog's fur. Under a microscope, he saw tiny hooks on the burs. This discovery directly inspired the invention of Velcro.
  * **Edible Root**: The taproot of the plant is edible and is popular in Japanese cuisine, where it is known as "gobo" and cooked in stir-fries.

### [IMG_5624.JPG](img/IMG_5624.JPG): Orchard Grass (*Dactylis glomerata*)
* **Common Name**: Orchard Grass / Cock's-foot
* **Scientific Name**: *Dactylis glomerata*
* **Description**: A coarse perennial bunchgrass growing 2 to 4 feet tall. It has grayish-green leaves and a highly distinctive panicle with spikelets grouped in dense, one-sided, flower-like clusters at the ends of its stiff branches.
* **Interesting Facts**:
  * **Cock's Foot**: The common name "cock's foot" in the UK comes from the shape of the seed head, which resembles a bird's foot.
  * **High Palatability**: It is widely cultivated for hay and pasture forage because it grows early in the season, is highly palatable to cattle and sheep, and tolerates shade better than most other pasture grasses.

### [IMG_5616.JPG](img/IMG_5616.JPG) & [IMG_5624.JPG](img/IMG_5624.JPG) & [IMG_5625.JPG](img/IMG_5625.JPG): Reed Canary Grass (*Phalaris arundinacea*)
* **Common Name**: Reed Canary Grass
* **Scientific Name**: *Phalaris arundinacea*
* **Description**: A tall, coarse, perennial wetland grass growing 3 to 9 feet tall. It has wide, flat, light-green leaves and a dense, straw-colored contracted panicle (seed head) that opens during flowering and closes afterwards.
* **Interesting Facts**:
  * **Wetland Monoculture**: While some strains are native, highly aggressive Eurasian cultivars were widely planted for agricultural forage and erosion control. It has become a dominant wetland invader, forming dense monocultures that choke out native sedges and cattails.
  * **Altered Waterways**: Due to its dense, thick sod-forming rhizomes, it can trap sediment and physically restrict water flow in small streams, ditches, and irrigation channels.

### [IMG_5623.JPG](img/IMG_5623.JPG) & [IMG_5624.JPG](img/IMG_5624.JPG) & [IMG_5625.JPG](img/IMG_5625.JPG): Bishop's Goutweed (*Aegopodium podagraria* 'Variegatum')
* **Common Name**: Variegated Bishop's Goutweed / Ground Elder
* **Scientific Name**: *Aegopodium podagraria* 'Variegatum'
* **Description**: A low-growing, deciduous perennial ground cover with compound, green leaves edged in creamy white. It produces small, white flower clusters (umbels) in late spring.
* **Interesting Facts**:
  * **Gout Remedy**: Historically introduced to Europe and North America by monks in the Middle Ages as a vegetable and medicinal herb to treat gout and arthritis (hence the name "goutweed").
  * **Underground Spreader**: It is highly invasive in forest understories and gardens, spreading rapidly through a dense, aggressive network of underground rhizomes. A tiny piece of root left in the ground can produce a whole new colony.
